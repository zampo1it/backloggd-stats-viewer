Completed

on

Windows PC
I love Silent Hill f. When I said it was a game that’s very important to me, I was still a bit afraid that I might be overhyping myself and end up disappointed in the end but that didn’t happen. I really took my time with it and looked at everything, because I wanted to enjoy every single moment. The story is phenomenal and fits Silent Hill perfectly. I also had fun with the combat system, it’s much more involved than in the original Silent Hill games, but I found it not as annoying as in the Silent Hill 2 remake. It even has some nice puzzles that, which sadly has become rare for the genre.
One thing I didn't liked where the survival horror mechanics themselves, you always have enough items and there’s never really a reason to be careful. It does have some neat ideas, like being able to trade certain items for faith, but overall it doesn’t really matter much. But honestly, i'm okay with that. The game gave me exactly what I wanted, a Silent Hill by Ryukishi07 and it absolutely delivered.
It’s the best Silent Hill in over 20 years, and the one game I can say without hesitation belongs right next to the original ones.

Shreto

Completed

on

Windows PC
My only real exposure to Silent Hill before this was Silent Hill 2, both the original and the remake. But I am the kind of person who does not really remember games I played a long time ago. Like, back in 5th grade I played Plants vs. Zombies, and when I tried revisiting it years later, it just did not feel the same. My perception was completely different. Same with Silent Hill 2. When I first played it as a kid, I did not remember the plot points or any of the iconic moments that people talk about. So whenever discussions came up, I could not really join in.

That is why when the remake was announced, I was genuinely excited. It meant I could finally experience the game again but with modern graphics, and all the trailers looked so promising. And honestly, when I played it, it was amazing. Sure, sometimes it got exhausting or confusing, but overall, I loved it.

And then, right after finishing it, I saw the announcement for Silent Hill F. My reaction was, I did not know what to expect. The trailers looked completely different from anything I had seen before. It was not like Silent Hill 2, so part of me was skeptical. To be very honest, I thought this was just a marketing move to set Silent Hill in Japan, something that would mostly cater to otakus and the degenerates who would be like, oh, look, a Japanese Silent Hill now.

Still, I had to play it. I wanted that same kind of vibe I got from Silent Hill 2, and I was not going to get it from replaying Resident Evil games I had already run through multiple times. So when Silent Hill F finally released, I jumped in. And trust me, it was completely the opposite of what I expected. It was genuinely brilliant.

Hinako Shimizu, sets the tone right away. It starts with her fighting with her father, followed by a family photo where her sister is dressed in a bridal gown, and her dad says, Why can’t you be more like her? That alone hinted to me that the game would explore her struggles against societal pressure.

As the story progressed, I met Shu, Rinko, and others, and saw how toxic the dynamics around Hinako were. Then comes the surreal other realm, and with it, the fox guy, Kotoyuki. He is from a noble family, tied to Hinako through an arranged marriage. From early on, it was clear he was being set up as that exaggerated savior figure, a satire of the whole damsel in distress trope.

I love that it's got great symbolic representation. The first ritual has Hinako losing her right arm. To me, that felt like a metaphor for losing agency, especially since most people are right handed. Then, when her face is carved into a fox mask, it symbolized the loss of her identity. Hinako ceases to exist as herself, and is now only the wife. Piece by piece, she is stripped of individuality, institutionalized into becoming like her sister and mother before her.

Even when she kills her friends in that realm, it is not literal, it represents her detaching from her past self, cutting ties with the life where she had freedom, opinions, and personality. Slowly, she is consumed by the role forced onto her.

The writing here is just brilliant. It is rich in symbolism, and it does not shy away from showing patriarchal trauma in rural Japan, and by extension, South and Southeast Asia. When Kotoyuki gives her the pin that marks her as his, it directly echoes real cultural practices, like the thali or sindoor in India, symbols of marriage that, at their core, can be seen as possessions. It is not direct objectification, but it is about ownership. Silent Hill F exaggerates and satirizes this beautifully, in a way that is both powerful and accessible. It is not some pretentious art film you need to decode for years. The metaphors are deep, but they are told so clearly that anyone can grasp them.

Later, the dual timeline is revealed. Hinako fights this grotesque bride like figure who killed her parents, only to realize it was her future self, the version of her that succumbed to the system, arranged marriage, and loss of agency. The game builds to a battle between these two versions of Hinako, essentially a fight for her freedom.

And then come the endings. This is not a case of one true ending and a bunch of throwaway side ones. Each ending feels deliberate, with unique bosses and rich storytelling. I have played three or four so far. In one, Shu transforms into this towering, monk like monster covered in faces. In another, Kotoyuki becomes a nine tailed fox. Each one feels insane, in the best way.

Beyond the writing, the game is gorgeous. The visuals are stunning, bloody, decayed, floral, surreal. The only real gripe I had was with some of the combat. Certain fights, especially towards the end, drag on and get repetitive. That giant flesh bag monster that spits out smaller ones, yeah, that got annoying fast. Fighting endless waves is not fun after a while. And honestly, I do not know how anyone manages hard mode for the action. Hard puzzles, sure. But hard combat feels masochistic.

Still, when it comes down to story, Silent Hill F honestly surpasses Silent Hill 2 in some ways. I know, Silent Hill 2 will always be the iconic one, but F stands tall in its own right. And considering it was written by Ryukishi07, the mind behind Higurashi and Umineko, it makes perfect sense. The man knows how to craft layered, symbolic, emotionally brutal stories.

This game shook me. I just got reminded of the fact that I've been playing this alongside titles like Expedition 33, SH2, RE4 etc, I think the gaming world is heading in a really exciting direction.

themriron2

on

Windows PC

again

Silent Hill f is interesting. I don't think the game quite realises its potential, but it has the bones of a top-class survival horror game. In short, here's what I think it did right and wrong. Before I say any of this, though, play through the game on story action difficulty. Don't make the mistake I did! "Hard" seems designed for returning players and is particularly challenging - it really exaggerates the gameplay grievances. "Story" misleads you in name, but it seems to be the intended difficulty for first-time players.

The good:
- Excellent acting performances in the original Japanese. Hinako's VA, in particular, delivers a hair-raising acting performance that greatly enhances the cutscenes in the game.
- Atmosphere. Though I think the nightmare world drags the atmosphere down, I think the overworld has great sensibility and mood. The Japanese aesthetic is well executed.
- Visual direction. The visual language of the game is excellent - cutscenes are very well-directed, mixing slow character shots with uncomfortable angles tastefully.
- Story. I think the story is good, even though I wasn't initially convinced. It is novel commentary not just on female life in 60s Japan, but the psychosis in this game leaves a satisfying amount of room for interpretation, particularly when other endings are considered.
- Sound design. While it isn't breaking any records, SHf does feature some great moments of sound design, which can convey things too horrible to show to the camera directly.

And here's where I think the game falters:
- Gameplay. Not a surprise, but SHf is just not particularly good to play. The game does not feel responsive. I agree with the argument that horror games shouldn't feel "fun" to play, but the core argument is that good horror gameplay should create tension. SHf does not. On hard it is a grating affair, as you battle the perceived latency of pressing anything at all only to lose half of your health as your stamina runs out. Story mode greatly alleviates this and is my recommended way to play. The in-game camera simply does not do it any favours either.
- Replay requirements. While I think the multiple endings does add value to the narrative, replaying the game 3 times to see all of the outcomes doesn't feel like the right way to handle it, considering the game doesn't play well enough to justify several playthroughs.
- Structure. I thought the game's nightmare sections ultimately didn't work well enough in execution. They veered quite combat-heavy - as did the later half of the main game - and could feel drawn-out affairs of basic puzzles and long sections of corridors. The game flipping between both the "real" and nightmare world slowed down the pacing considerably.
- Variety. There are scant few variations in enemy types seen after the opening acts of the game. It doesn't help the combat to feel engaging as the game throws the same enemies at you for the rest of the game, particularly as the placement and frequency of these enemies becomes silly later in the game. In general, the game feels like it repeats itself a lot both in terms of layouts and gameplay.
- Exploration. It is sad that the town is only half-realised in practice. In previous Silent Hill games, the impression of a town you can truly immerse in and explore added a great deal of credibility to the game's world, and was a hallmark of the older games. Not so here. Even in this beautiful town, you are herded like cattle through the corridors the game wants you to see, with lazy barriers like carts and trucks that mysteriously disappear when the game wants to bring you on a different route. The town does not feel like it was ever truly lived-in, but rather a collection of very pretty models, intended to be viewed only from the outside like billboards. It is a real shame that the town itself offers so little outside of the story required areas; it certainly feels like a loss compared to the exploration of classics like Silent Hill 2, where you could explore many shops and locations that existed primarily to boost your immersion and reward curiosity and investment.

Overall, I think SHf had the potential to be a modern classic, but makes too many key missteps to do so.

Athena02

Completed

on

Windows PC
Combat that is boring when not abysmal, mostly unimaginative psychological horror, a story that spends too much time on uninteresting ideas to let it's more noteworthy elements shine, and a take on the otherworld that falls completely flat.
The fogworld is where the game shines. Beautiful visuals, an interesting labyrinth of streets and alleys, fields and forest shrines, dense with detail and atmosphere. While chunks of the town are usually cut off in these sections you're always given plenty of opportunities to explore and find something interesting. The survival and combat mechanics show the most potential here, too, though still aren't "enjoyable" to a noteworthy degree. The beginning hours are where the game shows the most promise, wandering around the town, being able to flee enemies instead of being forced into combat, wandering about the mysteries of what's going on, it's a solid start.
My first major problem is with this games version of the otherworld, known in this game as the Dark Shrine. Compared to the fogworld, the dark shrine is embarrassingly simple in terms of level design, detail, atmosphere, pretty much everything. Where in previous games the otherworld was a chance to go even deeper into the world, deeper into the dark surrealism, deeper into the puzzles, deeper into the survival, here they feel like distractions. This isn't helped by the fact that the dark shrine is completely separate from the fogworld, whereas previous otherworlds were warped versions of levels you had already been exploring. The dark shrine has no atmosphere. This is not a surreal or dreamlike nightmare. It's a collection of modular assets put together to form uninteresting video game levels, that's all I could see it as.
The fogworld is also where the games lacking combat becomes unignorable. More often than not, fighting enemies is required in the dark shrine. On paper, the combat seems like it should be fine, but in execution it's almost unbearable. I think it's a number of "little things" that pile up to make it such an abysmal experience. It clearly understands that survival horror combat shouldn't be too smooth and the player shouldn't be too capable, but it's easy to let this slip into making the combat actually bad. The difference between these things is "fighting for your life in a desperate survival situation" vs "fussing with clunky video game systems". One is an experience, the other is frustration that leaves no imprint on a player. SHf falls firmly into the latter category.

This might sound like a nitpick but I couldn't stop thinking about it as I played. The game often blocks a path with lazy excuses, like a cart blocking a dirt path, even though you would easily be able to walk around it, or a small pile of boxes blocking a road. These are cases found in the level design that are simple excuses for why the player can't go wandering off in the wrong direction. The most offensive example though comes in the form of a small chainlink fence that blocks the characters on their journey. This happens in a cutscene. the characters simply cannot find a way of getting past this chainlink fence (which is only a little taller than them, has no barbed wire or anything hazardous) so they must take a massive detour through a school which then leads to the characters being split up, and all I can wonder is "why didn't they just climb over the fence?".
While lazy excuses like this might sometimes be acceptable for simple level design, having such illogical barriers be present in the narrative itself makes me question if the developers themselves are taking this experience seriously.
What makes it all the more annoying is that this is Silent Hill, you already have plenty of options for barring certain paths. Previous games had giant holes in the earth, large walls of fabric and metal, these solutions are great because they not only achieve the surface level goal of barring certain paths, but they serve as actual barriers that a person reasonably wouldn't be able to get past, and they play into the games surrealist identity and become iconic in their own right. What's doubly frustrating is that Silent Hill f has it's own version of this with the red ivy that spreads around, they sometimes use this to create genuine barriers, walls of red ivy and flowers that mimic flesh and tendons. That's it! You did it! It was that easy!

As for the narrative, it's too unfocused, repetitive, and unimaginative to really shine. Lots of cutscenes are slow, but not in an interesting way where thoughts are allowed to ferment and ideas unfurl in time, but rather, things just happen... slowly. It often gives way to boredom.
The dark shrine is also where repetition becomes a problem. Many of the interactions with the fox masked man are borderline the same thing happening over and over again. An air of mystery that takes far, far too long to actually lead to anything. There are high moments, interesting ideas that will stick with me, but the memory of those will always be tainted by them being attached to a story that doesn't do them justice.

Also the decision to turn the game into a hack and slash in the last quarter was a bold decision, but it struggles to work because it's filtered through survival horror mechanics. It's like if Resident Evil 2 tried to become Resident Evil 4 while still controlling exactly like Resident Evil 2.
